Annotations

Goals

When we look at a news article, we are often left with many questions, especially when it is a subject that we might not have been following closely in the past.

It is not reasonable to assume that every article should be written to the lowest common denominator. This would lead to something tedious to read once you were familiar with the subject matter. One possible approach to solving this is to provide annotations as a way to indicate that additional information is available.

Ideally, annotations would:

  • Not be overly intrusive or distracting.
  • Make additional information optionally available to the reader when it is useful, without blocking the overall flow of the article.
  • Be available and usable on all platforms.

Looking at the sample article that we are using for looking at the use and presentation of annotations, we can identify a number of open questions.

  • Why is Haditha Dam of strategic importance?
  • What other dams or targets are the Islamic State group going after?
  • How can we read more about what happened at the Mosul dam?
  • Who are the Peshmerga and what do they have to do with this story?
  • Is this operation by the United States part of the assistance in the "Baghdad Belt" that is mentioned?
  • Who is ISIL?

Some of these questions are clear to anyone who has been following the story, while others may not be. The importance of the dam is indirectly stated later in the article after it has been asserted to be of strategic or vital importance a couple of times.

Conclusions

While not yet complete with these experimental renderings, we are not happy with any of the approaches taken so far.

Tooltips can not be interacted with. This limits their usefulness as you can't dig deeper and explore multiple levels of annotations. That said, tooltips did work well within the mobile browser environment.

Popovers are interesting, but they also do not lend themselves to multiple levels of annotations. They also do not work well in the mobile browser environment where the screen may be fairly small.

Margin notes could work nicely within the desktop browser environment. However, they do not work well in a space-constrained mobile environment. They also require that the margin be left clear so that the margin notes have somewhere to live. Many news websites, for better or worse, prefer to put a lot of additional material in the margins to draw readers to other reporting.

Additionally, we felt that there were some other issues with the approach of using annotations as a whole.

  • The amount of text that can be given for each item is very limited. It must be tightly edited and restricted to only the most salient facts.
  • Annotations do not handle the idea of nested or layered annotations very well. They are not a good entry point to a non-linear narrative. They are only well suited for very brief explanations of a term or phrase.

We feel that some aspects of this problem may be altered by using "storylines" and will pursue that avenue of investigation next.

We will also continue to think about this problem and consider alternative approaches.
